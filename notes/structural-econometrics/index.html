From Choices to Counterfactuals

Structural Estimation for Digital Platform Research
Discrete choice, demand estimation, and counterfactuals

These notes are written for empirical researchers who study digital platforms and information systems. They follow one continuous thread: what a user's discrete-choice data reveals about preferences, how to estimate those preferences, and how to use the estimated model to answer questions that experiments and regressions cannot. What would a policy that has never been tried actually do? Where do churned users go? How much welfare does a design change give a user, or take away?

The nine chapters follow that thread. The first two explain when a structural model is really needed and give a framework for choosing one. From there the series starts with random utility and logit, gradually relaxes the restrictions on substitution and admits preference heterogeneity, works through the numerical details of simulation-based estimation, tackles price endogeneity and BLP, and reaches dynamic discrete choice. It closes with close readings of canonical platform-research papers and a workflow that runs from research question to finished paper, model validation included. The cases and data used for illustration are not real; each is marked as such in the text.
