Foundations of Information Systems Economics

Empirical Methods for Information Systems Economics

When you study digital platforms and artificial intelligence, the hard part is rarely a shortage of methods. It is that the theory, the identification strategy, and the institutional mechanism all have to make sense at once. These notes are written for researchers in information systems economics and empirical operations management, with an emphasis on the business uses of AI, digital platforms, information environments, technology adoption, and the market behavior of consumers and firms. The whole book proceeds from research questions. We first use graduate microeconomic theory to write a real-world puzzle as a problem of choice, equilibrium, and incentives; then use microeconometrics and causal inference to judge what the data can actually identify; then bring in empirical industrial organization and structural econometrics to analyze substitution, competitive conduct, and counterfactual policy; and finally carry these tools back into the concrete institutions of information, technology, and platform economics. The goal is not to make you an expert in one method, but to build a research capability with both depth and horizontal reach: substantive expertise in information, AI, platforms, and technology economics, together with the theoretical, econometric, and computational language to work alongside economists, marketing scholars, and operations researchers.
